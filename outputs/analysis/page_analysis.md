## Main Topic
Supply and demand is a fundamental economic model of price determination in a market. It postulates that, holding all else equal, the unit price for a particular good or other traded item in a perfectly competitive market will vary until it settles at the market-clearing price, where the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied, achieving economic equilibrium. The concept forms the theoretical basis of modern economics.

**Key Concepts**

**1. Supply Schedule (Supply Curve)**
- A table showing the relationship between price and quantity supplied by producers
- Under perfect competition, supply is determined by marginal cost
- Firms produce additional output as long as marginal cost is less than market price
- Supply curve shifts:
  - Leftward shift: Rise in production costs (decreased supply)
  - Rightward shift: Fall in production costs (increased supply)
- Mathematical representations:
  - Linear supply function
  - Constant-elasticity (isoelastic) supply function
- Distinguish between individual firm supply and market supply (sum of individual supplies)
- Short-run vs. long-run supply curves (long-run is flatter/more elastic)

**2. Demand Schedule (Demand Curve)**
- Shows amount buyers are willing and able to purchase at various prices
- Law of demand: demand curve is always downward-sloping (as price decreases, quantity demanded increases)
- Consumers buy additional units while marginal value exceeds market price
- Exceptions to the law of demand:
  - Veblen goods: More attractive at higher prices (fashion, signaling)
  - Giffen goods: Inferior goods that absorb large portion of income (e.g., staple foods)
- Mathematical representations:
  - Linear demand function
  - Constant-elasticity demand function
- Convention: Price on vertical y-axis, quantity on horizontal x-axis

**3. Equilibrium**
- Equilibrium: Price-quantity pair where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied
- Represented by intersection of demand and supply curves
- Market clears at this point
- Market equilibrium analysis is fundamental to microeconomics

**4. Changes in Market Equilibrium**

**Demand Curve Shifts:**
- Increase in demand: Curve shifts right, increases both equilibrium price (P1→P2) and quantity (Q1→Q2)
- Decrease in demand: Curve shifts left, decreases both equilibrium price and quantity
- Demand shift factors: changing tastes, incomes, prices of complements/substitutes, expectations, number of buyers

**Supply Curve Shifts:**
- Increase in supply: Curve shifts right, decreases equilibrium price but increases quantity
- Decrease in supply: Curve shifts left, increases equilibrium price but decreases quantity
- Supply shift factors: technological progress, changes in production costs, resource availability

**5. Partial Equilibrium**
- Analysis considering only part of the market
- Prices of all other products held fixed
- Simpler than general equilibrium model
- Assumes other markets have little impact
- Useful in constricted markets

### Other Markets

**Money Market**
- Supply and demand model applies to money market
- Interest rate is the "price"
- Money supply can be vertical (fixed by central bank) or horizontal (targeting interest rate)

**Biological Markets**
- Laws of supply and demand apply to behavior of social animals and all living things
- Metabolic systems: feedback inhibition allows pathways to respond to demand

### Empirical Estimation
- Demand and supply relations can be statistically estimated
- Uses simultaneous-equation methods in econometrics
- Structural estimation vs. reduced-form estimation
- Parameter identification problem requires exogenous variable data

### Macroeconomic Uses
- Aggregate demand-aggregate supply model relates to total output and aggregate price level
- Used to relate money supply/demand to interest rates
- Used to relate labor supply/demand to wage rates
- Sonnenschein-Mantel-Debreu theorem shows aggregate demand may not inherit individual rationality properties

### History

**Ancient/Early References**
- Tirukkural (256th couplet, ~2000 years ago): "If the world desireth not meat for eating, there will be none to offer it for sale"
- Ibn Taymiyyah (14th century Muslim scholar): Understood that prices rise when desire increases and availability decreases

**Modern Development**
- 1767: James Denham-Steuart first used phrase "supply and demand" in his "Inquiry into the Principles of Political Economy"
- 1776: Adam Smith used the phrase in "The Wealth of Nations"
- 1803: Thomas Robert Malthus used phrase 20 times in "Essay on Population"
- 1838: Augustin Cournot first drew demand curves
- 1870: Fleeming Jenkin published first drawing of supply and demand curves in English, introduced comparative statics
- 1890: Alfred Marshall developed and popularized the model in "Principles of Economics"
- Late 19th century: Marginalist school emerged (Jevons, Menger, Walras)

### Criticism

**Sraffa's Critique**
- Focused on inconsistency of partial equilibrium analysis
- Questioned rationale for upward slope of supply curve in market for produced consumption good

**Post-Keynesian Criticism**
- Model fails to explain prevalence of administered prices
- Retail prices set by firms based on mark-up over costs, not responsive to demand changes

## Summary

This Wikipedia article provides a comprehensive overview of supply and demand, one of the most fundamental concepts in economics. The article covers:

**Core Theory**: Supply and demand is an economic model explaining how prices are determined in competitive markets through the interaction of producers (supply) and consumers (demand). The market clears at equilibrium where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

**Key Components**:
- **Supply Curve**: Shows relationship between price and quantity producers supply, determined by marginal cost, generally upward-sloping
- **Demand Curve**: Shows relationship between price and quantity consumers demand, generally downward-sloping (Law of Demand)
- **Equilibrium**: Intersection point where market clears

**Dynamics**: The article explains how equilibrium changes when either curve shifts due to factors like technological progress (supply shifts), changing incomes or tastes (demand shifts), etc.

**Applications**: The model applies to various markets including:
- Goods markets (primary focus)
- Money markets (interest rates)
- Labor markets (wage rates)
- Biological systems (metabolic pathways)

**Historical Development**: Traces the concept from ancient wisdom (Tirukkural) through early Islamic scholars (Ibn Taymiyyah) to formal economic theory (Steuart, Smith, Cournot, Jenkin, Marshall, and the marginalist school).

**Limitations**: The article notes when the model doesn't apply - when firms have market power (monopoly, oligopoly) or buyers have monopsony power, requiring more complex models. Also notes theoretical criticisms from Post-Keynesians about the prevalence of administered prices in real markets.

The article serves as an educational foundation for understanding basic microeconomics and the mechanisms of price determination in market economies.
